"#commentary/sp_a1_begin/com-ride.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] Starting a Portal 2 mod can be quite challenging. We want it to be unique, but also not too out there so that players wouldn't recognize it as Portal. Early iterations featured a test chamber vault just like in Portal 1, but we felt like it was too derivative. The container ride we ended up with is very reminiscent of Portal 2, but has a few twists. In Portal 2, the ride is highly dynamic and epic, whereas here it's rather uneventful and more subtle in its world building. We wanted to show the player the state of our world right off the bat. The slow ride in the dimly lit Aperture facility shows that we're in a low-power environment, that is still largely functional. The facility operates as intended. Just another human being dragged out of their cold storage vault and sent to the test chambers to die."
"#commentary/sp_a1_begin/com-hert.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] Early play testers often reported that they didn't know why they were solving test chambers. We thought in a Portal mod it would be obvious why you're solving puzzles, but the lack of a goal was still an issue. In the original Portal, players don't really have a goal spelled out to them either, but the mystery surrounding Aperture and GLaDOS is itself enough of a motivator. Players naturally ask “Who is this robot voice? Why are all the observation rooms empty? What am I testing for” That worked for Portal, not so much for a mod. This is where the human emergency response team comes into play. It is presented as some sort of elite crew of highly skilled test subjects, which the player tries to join through solving difficult and dangerous puzzles. This immediately proved successful. It's a vague goal and eventually turns out to be a lie anyway, but it keeps players engaged enough for the first chapter until their real mission begins."

"#commentary/sp_a1_begin/com-strata.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] This will come up again and again so I want to clear up the organisational structure of Strata source to clear up any confusion. Two other modders and I originally got source code for Portal 2, CS:GO and a bunch of other Valve games back in 2020 to develop a mod called Portal 2: Community Edition, which is still in development and intended to be an improved base for Portal 2 mods. I worked on Revolution and P2CE side by side, intending to use the P2CE engine for my own Revolution mod. Later we were joined by Momentum mod and founded what is now known as Strata Source. A shared engine branch used by P2CE, Momentum and Revolution. We all use the same engine and can share features, but also all make our own custom tweaks to each game. Collaboration is at the heart of Strata and allows smaller mods like Revolution, which don't have many programmers on its team, to inherit features shared by the other mods. For example, our DirectX 11 port and some graphical features like parallax corrected cubemaps originated largely in Momentum and have been added to the base engine so we can benefit from that work as well. Other features like improved level loading speed have been contributed by P2CE developers. And some of the features we added to Revolution are now also available in P2CE and Momentum. So if you're ever confused about what Strata and P2CE are. It's just a shared codebase that allows modders to share a common engine. "

"#commentary/sp_a1_cube_fling_intro/com-one_space.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] The source engine loads all level data into memory at once and requires loading screens to transition to the next level. So a question you will eventually have to answer is when to place a loading screen. Portal 2 maps were typically very short, often featuring only one test chamber per map. This is no problem for large chambers that take some time to solve, but Portal 2 is sometimes criticized  for having very frequent loading screens. If you increase the size of each map, you end up with less loading screens, but each one will take longer and the game will use up more memory. Rather than splitting maps according to file size or entity count, we used them as pace breaks. This map includes 4 small test chambers that all build on each other. Bundling them together also allows us to connect the chambers in more interesting ways. Here they all share the same space, and can be visited through the observation rooms at the end. Loading screens are often hated, but we found they work well as natural points for players to take a break or stop playing."

"#commentary/sp_a1_cube_fling_intro/com-pcc.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] Source uses cubemaps to simulate reflections, but they always had one massive flaw. They appeared to move with the player's camera. "#commentary/sp_a2_outside/com-csm.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] Portal 2 uses projected textures to create sunlight with real-time shadows in its overgrown areas. Community mappers do the same but the technique isn't ideal. Projected textures are spotlights and create converging shadows, which is not how sun shadows look. Their resolution is also limited, so the further you stretch their area of influence, the worse the shadows look. They don't follow the player and if you try to parent it to the player, the shadows will look wrong when you look through a portal. Long story short, projected textures aren't ideal for sunlight. CS:GO implemented a similar shadow-mapping technique built specifically for sunlight called Cascaded Shadow Maps or CSM. Since our custom engine branch is based on CS:GO, we were able to use it in Portal: Revolution."
"#commentary/sp_a2_outside/com-csm_portals.wav" "[Stefan Heinz] CSM works by casting 3 shadow maps, called cascades, around the player from the same angle, but on different scales. The first cascade is just a few meters around the player, the next a few more and the last reaches as far as a player would be expected to see in a typical map. This means that shadows will always be at high resolution around the player and low resolution further away where it doesn't matter. Now as always, portals complicate everything. Since the shadows follow the player, if you look through a portal, the shadow resolution will still be centered around the player's position. This caused awful shadows while looking through a portal and popping when you step through and have your position updated. This took a long time to fix. We were first aware of this bug around 2020 and have only been able to fix it in late 2023. We needed to add an additional 3 cascades centered around the position of the camera behind the portal from where the portal view is being rendered. I'm sure there are many ways to further optimize this, since this technique now needs to render twice as many shadowmaps, but we were simply happy to have finally fixed this issue that has been bugging us for years."
